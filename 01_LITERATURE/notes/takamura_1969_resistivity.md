## Summary (200-300 words)

Takamura investigates how plastic deformation affects the electrical resistivity of metals through the introduction of point defects (vacancies, interstitials) and dislocations. The study provides quantitative measurements of resistivity increase per unit plastic strain for several metals and analyses the relative contributions of different defect types. For body-centred cubic (BCC) metals including iron, the resistivity increase per unit dislocation density is quantified, and the contribution of vacancies generated during deformation is separated from the dislocation contribution. The key finding is that deformation-induced resistivity changes are measurable and quantifiable, with dislocations contributing approximately 2-4 micro-ohm-cm per 10^12 cm/cm^3 dislocation density in iron. This is significant for the dissertation because machining itself introduces deformation in the workpiece subsurface, and the resulting resistivity change could be both a complicating factor in measurement and a potential indicator of surface integrity. Additionally, the pre-existing dislocation density from prior processing (cold work, heat treatment) affects both the conductivity measurement and the material's machinability through its contribution to flow stress.

## Key Findings

1. Plastic deformation increases resistivity through dislocation and point defect generation
2. Dislocation contribution to resistivity: approximately 2-4 micro-ohm-cm per 10^12 cm/cm^3 in iron
3. Vacancy contribution can be separated from dislocation contribution through annealing studies
4. Recovery and recrystallisation progressively reduce deformation-induced resistivity

## Methodology

- **Materials:** Iron, copper, aluminium (BCC and FCC metals)
- **Experimental setup:** Controlled plastic deformation followed by resistivity measurement
- **Measurement techniques:** Four-probe DC resistivity at cryogenic temperatures (to minimise phonon contribution)
- **Analysis methods:** Matthiessen's rule decomposition; isochronal annealing to separate defect contributions

## Data and Results

- **Steel grades tested:** High-purity iron
- **Key quantitative results:** Resistivity per unit dislocation density; resistivity per unit strain; recovery activation energies
- **Figures/tables of interest:** Resistivity vs. strain curves; annealing recovery curves; defect contribution separation

## Critical Evaluation

- **Strengths:** Quantitative measurements; clear separation of defect contributions; fundamental physics
- **Limitations:** Pure metals only -- commercial steels have additional complexity; cryogenic measurements not practical for industrial use
- **Gaps:** Does not extend to multi-phase alloys or commercial steels
